HOW THE N55 HPFP MSV WORKS
---------------------------
The N55 uses a Bosch HDP5-family high-pressure pump driven by a 3-lobe cam.
The Metering Solenoid Valve (MSV) is a normally-OPEN spill valve. When
de-energized, fuel spills back and no pressure builds. When energized during
the compression stroke, the valve closes, trapping and pressurizing fuel for
delivery to the rail.

Control architecture:
  • Feedforward path: open-loop estimate of cam angle needed to deliver the
    requested fuel mass (Gain × demand + Offset)
  • PID loop: closed-loop correction on actual vs. target rail pressure
  • Current control: peak-and-hold driver — high pull-in current snaps the
    valve shut, lower hold current keeps it closed throughout compression

The DS25/HDP6 + 38% lift kit increases per-stroke pump displacement by
roughly 3–4× vs. stock. This fundamentally changes every parameter the
DME uses to control the pump.

CHANGE 1 — MSV Maximum Delivery Angle
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1C9F52
Size     : u16 LE (2 bytes)
XDF name : MSV Maximum Delivery Angle

  ORIG : 40  (raw)
  MOD  :  0  (raw)

WHAT IT DOES:
  Caps the maximum cam-angle window during which the DME can activate the MSV.
  A larger angle = more fuel delivered per stroke. This was a hard ceiling to
  prevent over-pressurization on stock hardware.

WHY IT CHANGED:
  Setting to 0 in Bosch MEVD calibration convention = NO LIMIT enforced.
  The function is bypassed, letting the PID and feedforward operate freely.
  The DS25 + 38% lift cam needs the full angular range available — the old
  ceiling of 40 units would artificially restrict delivery well below the
  upgraded pump's capability.

VERDICT: Correct. Limit removed to unlock full DS25 delivery range.

CHANGE 2 — Max. Relative Fuel Mass for HPFP Plausibility
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1CD46C
Size     : 4 × u16 LE = 8 bytes
XDF name : Max. relative fuel mass for HPFP plausibility

  ORIG : [5333, 6400, 6400, 6400]
  MOD  : [15999, 19200, 19200, 19200]
  Ratio: exactly 3.0× across all cells

WHAT IT DOES:
  The DME computes how much fuel the HPFP is delivering (based on MSV command
  angle, cam speed, pump geometry) and cross-checks it against injector demand.
  If HPFP appears to deliver more than this ceiling, it flags a plausibility
  fault (DTC 29F1 — Fuel Pressure Plausibility).

WHY IT CHANGED:
  The DS25 + 38% lift cam delivers roughly 3× the volumetric flow of stock at
  the same cam angle. The stock ceiling would be blown through constantly,
  triggering false fault codes and potential limp mode even when the system is
  working correctly. The 3× multiplier exactly tracks the hardware's increased
  flow capability.

VERDICT: Correct and necessary. Without this change, the car would throw
29F1 under any real fuel demand with the upgraded pump.

CHANGE 3 — HPFP P-Factor and I-Factor (PID Gains)
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1D0B4C
Size     : 6 × u16 LE = 12 bytes (P and I tables share this region)
XDF name : HPFP I-Factor / HPFP P-Factor

  ORIG : [22118, 9830, 7373, 7373, 9830, 22118]
  MOD  : [15483, 6881, 5161, 5161, 6881, 15483]
  Ratio: 0.700 × across every cell (exactly 30% reduction)

WHAT IT DOES:
  Proportional and integral gains of the closed-loop PID controller that
  adjusts MSV activation angle to maintain target rail pressure.

WHY IT CHANGED (the counterintuitive part):
  Reducing PID gain for a more powerful pump seems backwards, but it's correct.
  The DS25 + lift kit delivers ~3× more fuel per unit of cam-angle change.
  Applying stock gains to a plant with 3× higher gain causes aggressive
  oscillation — a small pressure error triggers a correction that delivers
  WAY too much fuel, reversing the error and oscillating. This is well-
  documented on N55 HPFP upgrade threads (SpoolStreet: "Dorch 2.5 HPFP
  Rail Pressure Oscillations"). The 30% gain reduction re-stabilizes the
  loop by accounting for the pump's increased authority per degree.

NOTE: The symmetric pattern [22118, 9830, 7373 | 7373, 9830, 22118] mirrors
  exactly, suggesting this is a 3-cell table with RPM-zone indexing (low/mid/hi
  RPM in two banks). The uniform 70% scaling preserved the relative shape.

VERDICT: Correct. Stability fix — prevents rail pressure hunting/oscillation.

CHANGE 4 — MSV Opening Delay (Battery Voltage)
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1D4070
Size     : 4 × u16 LE = 8 bytes (indexed by battery voltage, high→low V)
XDF name : MSV Opening Delay (Battery Voltage)

  ORIG : [1850, 1650, 1450, 1400]  (at ~14V, ~13V, ~12V, ~11V approx)
  MOD  : [2300, 2200, 2100, 2000]
  Delta: +450, +550, +650, +600 μs (if units are μs — at lower voltage the
         delay increase is proportionally larger, which is physically correct)

WHAT IT DOES:
  Compensates for solenoid response latency across battery voltage. At lower
  voltage, current rises more slowly (V = L·dI/dt), so the valve opens later
  than commanded. The DME pre-compensates by advancing the command by this
  delay value so the actual opening occurs at the correct cam angle.

WHY IT CHANGED:
  The DS25 solenoid coil has different electrical characteristics (inductance,
  resistance) than the stock HDP5 solenoid. The new solenoid takes longer to
  physically respond to the same electrical stimulus. Without correction,
  the valve opens late in the cam stroke, reducing delivery per stroke and
  causing inconsistent rail pressure — especially at idle and cold start.
  The larger delay corrections at lower voltages are physically correct:
  slower current rise at low voltage → even more delay needed.

VERDICT: Correct. Essential for reliable valve actuation with new solenoid.

CHANGE 5 — MSV Minimum Hold Current
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1D4224
Size     : u8 (1 byte)
XDF name : MSV Minimum Hold Current

  ORIG : 67  (raw)
  MOD  : 75  (raw)
  Delta: +8 units (+11.9%)

WHAT IT DOES:
  Floor for the "hold" phase of peak-and-hold solenoid control. After the
  high pull-in current snaps the valve shut, the driver drops to this lower
  hold current to maintain closure with less heat.

WHY IT CHANGED:
  At higher rail pressures (the DS25 supports up to 250 bar, the lift kit
  pushes more volume per stroke) and with a more aggressive cam profile,
  the back-pressure force on the closed valve is greater. A higher minimum
  hold current ensures the solenoid generates enough magnetic force to keep
  the valve sealed throughout the compression stroke. If hold current is too
  low, the valve partially opens mid-stroke, dumping fuel back and causing
  delivery inconsistency and pressure drop.

VERDICT: Correct. Higher holding force needed to resist elevated compression.

CHANGE 6 — MSV Pull-in Current Lower Limit (Untere Grenze Anzugsstrom)
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1D4235
Size     : 30 × u8 = 30 bytes
XDF name : Untere Grenze des Anzugsstroms des MSV

  ORIG : [100, 108, 113, 113, 108, 100, 108, 113, 113, 108,
           100, 108, 113, 113, 108, 108, 120, 127, 133, 118,
           113, 123, 130, 135, 120, 113, 125, 133, 138, 123]
  MOD  : [157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157,
           157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157,
           157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157, 157]

WHAT IT DOES:
  Lower bound on the "peak" (pull-in) current pulse that snaps the solenoid
  valve closed. The stock table varied this across operating conditions (RPM,
  temperature, load).

WHY IT CHANGED (flat + raised):
  Two things happened simultaneously:
  1. RAISED: The DS25 solenoid needs more pull-in force to reliably snap shut
     given its different magnetic path and spring characteristics. The floor
     was moved from the 100–138 range to 157 (>+24% increase minimum).
  2. FLATTENED: When characterizing a new solenoid whose behavior across
     all operating conditions isn't yet fully mapped, using a single
     conservative (high) value ensures reliable closure in EVERY cell.
     It trades a small amount of thermal efficiency for guaranteed robustness.
     This is standard re-characterization practice — map the worst case,
     iterate later with data logging.

VERDICT: Correct conservative calibration for a new solenoid assembly.

CHANGE 7 — MfVD Codeword
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1D427E
Size     : u8 (1 byte)
XDF name : MfVD Codeword

  ORIG : 191 = 0xBF = 0b10111111
  MOD  : 190 = 0xBE = 0b10111110
  Change: Bit 0 (LSB) CLEARED

WHAT IT DOES:
  "MfVD" = Kraftstoffmasse (fuel mass) Vorsteuerung Direkt (direct feedforward).
  Codewords in Bosch MEVD17.2 are 8-bit registers where each bit enables or
  disables a specific monitoring or control sub-function. Bit 0 of this
  codeword likely controls a fuel mass cross-check or delivery symmetry
  monitoring function in the direct injection feedforward path.

WHY IT CHANGED:
  Clearing bit 0 disables one monitoring sub-function that was calibrated
  for stock pump behavior. With the DS25's different delivery profile,
  this monitor would fire false alarms or impose an unintended constraint —
  similar to the plausibility limit in Change 2 but for a different check.
  Without access to the DAMOS/A2L symbol file this bit cannot be named
  precisely, but the direction (disabling a constraint) is consistent with
  all other changes in this tune.

VERDICT: Consistent with the broader pattern of disabling stock-calibrated
monitors that would false-trigger with upgraded HPFP hardware.

CHANGE 8 — MSV Feedforward Offset
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1D6CE0
Size     : 95 bytes total (shown here as u16 LE values)
XDF name : MSV Feedforward Offset

  ORIG : [5, 20, 35, 55, 60, 65,   5, 20, 35, 55, 60, 65,  10, 20, 35, ...]
  MOD  : [1,  5,  9, 14, 15, 16,   1,  5,  9, 14, 15, 16,   2,  5,  9, ...]
  Ratio: ~4× reduction across all cells (pattern shape preserved)

WHAT IT DOES:
  Additive bias term in the feedforward path — a fixed offset added to the
  base model before the gain is applied. It compensates for systematic
  under-delivery of the stock pump relative to its theoretical model.

WHY IT CHANGED:
  The stock offset was trimmed up to compensate for the HDP5's tendency to
  deliver slightly less fuel than the model predicted (pumps have volumetric
  efficiency losses, leakage past the valve, etc.). The DS25 has better
  volumetric efficiency and higher per-stroke delivery — it already
  over-delivers relative to the stock model. Keeping the positive bias offset
  would cause the feedforward to command even more delivery than needed,
  forcing the PID into constant negative (pull-back) correction. Reducing
  the offset brings the feedforward baseline into alignment with the DS25's
  actual delivery characteristics, letting the PID operate symmetrically.

NOTE: The shape (small offset at low demand → larger at high demand) is
  preserved — only the magnitude was scaled down ~4×. This is methodical
  re-scaling, not a reset to zero.

VERDICT: Correct. Baseline delivery re-centered for upgraded pump efficiency.

CHANGE 9 — MSV Feedforward Gain
================================================================================
Address  : 0x1D6D66
Size     : 8 × u16 LE = 16 bytes (indexed by RPM, low→high)
XDF name : MSV Feedforward Gain

  ORIG : [6594, 6594, 6416, 6171, 6063, 5993, 5505, 5160]
  MOD  : [1655, 1655, 1606, 1540, 1507, 1196,  819,  492]

  Cell-by-cell reduction factors:
    RPM cell 0: 6594 / 1655 = 3.98×
    RPM cell 1: 6594 / 1655 = 3.98×
    RPM cell 2: 6416 / 1606 = 3.99×
    RPM cell 3: 6171 / 1540 = 4.01×
    RPM cell 4: 6063 / 1507 = 4.02×
    RPM cell 5: 5993 / 1196 = 5.01×  ← higher RPM, more aggressive reduction
    RPM cell 6: 5505 /  819 = 6.72×
    RPM cell 7: 5160 /  492 = 10.5×  ← highest RPM, ~10× reduction

WHAT IT DOES:
  The proportional multiplier in the feedforward path. Scales how aggressively
  a given fuel mass request is translated into cam-angle activation command.

WHY IT CHANGED (the most dramatic and sophisticated change):
  The DS25 + 38% lift cam delivers dramatically more fuel per unit of cam
  angle than stock. Stock gain calibration ensured the feedforward alone
  delivered approximately the right amount. With a 3–4× more capable pump,
  the same gain causes massive rail pressure overshoot — the feedforward
  commands far too much fuel before the PID can respond.

  The ~4× reduction at low-mid RPM (cells 0–4) reflects the pump's increased
  displacement per stroke. At high RPM (cells 5–7), the reduction is even
  steeper (5–10×) because:
  - The cam spins faster → more pump strokes per second → fuel delivery
    per unit of activation time is exponentially higher
  - The DME must command proportionally shorter activation windows
  - Over-delivery at high RPM is harder to correct quickly with the PID
    (less time between strokes), so the feedforward must be even more
    conservative to avoid rail pressure spikes

  This is the most technically sophisticated change in the tune. The
  graduated reduction factor (4× → 10×) across RPM cells shows the tuner
  understood the nonlinear relationship between pump speed, per-stroke
  delivery, and required feedforward authority.

VERDICT: Correct. The magnitude and RPM-progressive reduction are physically
justified and consistent with Dorch DS25 + 38% lift flow capacity data.

OVERALL ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

This is a clean, complete, and technically sound HPFP re-characterization.
The tuner addressed every aspect of MSV control that changes when you install
a Dorch DS25 + 38% lift kit:

  Control limits     → raised/removed to accommodate higher flow (Changes 1, 2)
  PID stability      → gains reduced 30% to prevent oscillation (Change 3)
  Solenoid timing    → opening delay increased for new solenoid (Change 4)
  Solenoid current   → hold and pull-in currents raised (Changes 5, 6)
  Fault monitoring   → one stock-calibrated check disabled (Change 7)
  Feedforward path   → gain and offset re-centered for DS25 output (Changes 8, 9)

WHAT THIS TUNE DOES NOT INCLUDE:
  This is only the HPFP hardware enablement tune. It does NOT include:
  - Fuel (lambda) table changes for E85
  - Ignition timing changes
  - Boost / WGDC changes
  - Torque limit changes
  - Injector compensation
  Those remain for your E85 build (E85v2.bin / E85v3.bin workflow).

VALIDATION RECOMMENDATIONS:
  After flashing this (or integrating its HPFP changes into your E85 bin):
  1. Log rail pressure at WOT — should be stable without oscillation
  2. Log actual vs. commanded rail pressure — delta should be <5 bar steady state
  3. No DTC 29F1 (fuel pressure plausibility) under any load condition
  4. Check MHD live data: HPFP duty cycle should be in a reasonable range
     (not pegged at max, not oscillating)

INTEGRATION NOTE:
  The 9 address ranges from LKS25 should be applied on top of your E85v2.bin
  before building the E85v3.bin map slots. The HPFP calibration is separate
  from and compatible with fuel/timing/boost table changes.
